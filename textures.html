LESSON 7: TEXTURES

Visual and Tactile Surfaces, Both Simulated Digitally and Created Traditionally

 

Introduction: The Skin of the World

Texture is the surface quality of an object. It tells the viewer what something would feel like if they could reach out and touch it. Is it rough like tree bark? Smooth like polished marble? Soft like velvet? Sharp like broken glass?

Texture serves three critical functions in art:

  1. Realism: Texture grounds an object in the physical world. A dragon without scales is a balloon. A tree without bark is a green lollipop.
  2. Contrast: Texture provides a resting place for the eye. A highly textured area (a rocky cliff) makes a smooth area (a calm sky) feel even smoother by comparison.
  3. Emotion: Textures carry psychological weight. Rough textures feel ancient, dangerous, or weathered. Smooth textures feel futuristic, pristine, or cold. Soft textures feel comforting, intimate, or vulnerable.

This lesson will teach you how to see texture as a pattern of value and edge, and how to create it using both traditional tools and digital brushes.

 

SECTION 1: THE THEORY OF TEXTURE – Seeing Like an Artist

Before you can draw or paint a texture, you must understand what creates it visually.

1.1 The Three Components of Visual Texture

ComponentDefinitionVisual Example
Value ContrastThe difference between the lightest and darkest parts of the texture.High Contrast: Shiny metal, wet surfaces, broken glass. Low Contrast: Matte paper, fog, worn denim.
Edge QualityHow sharp or soft the transitions are between the texture elements.Hard Edges: Cracks in rock, wood grain lines, hair strands. Soft Edges: Moss, clouds, brushed suede.
Pattern DensityHow tightly packed the repeating elements are.High Density: Fine sand, pebbled leather, skin pores. Low Density: Cobblestones, large scales, bark chunks.

The Golden Rule of Rendering Texture: "Texture is most visible in the halftone (the transition between light and shadow). It disappears in the brightest highlight and the deepest shadow."

Implication: Do not apply texture evenly over the entire object. Concentrate your texture work in the mid-value areas. The highlight should be smooth (blown out). The core shadow should be smooth (crushed black). This is what makes a sphere look like a shiny sphere vs. a fuzzy sphere.

1.2 The Edge of the Silhouette (The "Bristle" Effect)

Texture does not only exist inside the object. It affects the outer contour. Smooth object: perfectly clean continuous outline. Textured object: broken, jagged, or fuzzy outline. When drawing a furry animal, do not draw a smooth outline and then add fur inside – the fur creates the outline. Draw the silhouette as a series of small, repeated strokes that break the boundary.

 

SECTION 2: TRADITIONAL TEXTURE TECHNIQUES (The Mark-Making Vocabulary)

Traditional media excels at tactile texture—texture you can actually feel with your fingers.

2.1 Dry Media (Graphite, Charcoal, Pastel)

  • Hatching: Parallel lines. Density controls value. Use: smooth shading, wood grain, directional hair.
  • Cross-Hatching: Overlapping lines at an angle. Use: deep shadows, rough cloth, weathered metal.
  • Stippling: Dots. The closer the dots, the darker the value. Use: sand, stone, skin pores, fine mist.
  • Scumbling / Circulism: Small, overlapping circular scribbles. Use: soft textures like clouds, foliage, or smooth skin gradients.
  • Lifting / Erasing: Using a kneaded eraser to remove graphite. Use: highlights on hair, light reflecting off water ripples, dust motes.

The "Paper Tooth" Principle: Rough paper "grabs" charcoal, creating grain automatically. Smooth paper allows seamless blending. Choose your paper based on the texture you want.

2.2 Wet Media (Ink, Watercolor, Acrylic)

  • Dry Brush: Load brush with little paint, drag across rough paper. Use: wood grain, weathered bone, distant grass.
  • Spattering: Flicking paint off a stiff brush. Use: stars, spray from a wave, dirt, rust, freckles.
  • Salt on Watercolor: Sprinkle salt onto wet wash. Salt absorbs pigment, creating crystalline blooms. Use: snow, ice, lichen, magical energy.
  • Plastic Wrap / Cling Film: Press plastic wrap onto wet wash, let dry, remove. Creates angular, faceted pools. Use: cracked earth, ice floes, abstract backgrounds.
  • Lifting Wet-in-Wet: Dropping clean water or blotting into a wet wash. Use: soft clouds, smoke, soft shadow edges.
  • Impasto (Acrylic/Oil): Applying paint thickly with palette knife or stiff brush so it stands up from canvas. Use: actual tactile texture – thick snow, heavy oil strokes, bread crust.

2.3 Rubbing and Frottage

Placing paper over a textured surface and rubbing with graphite or crayon. Surfaces: wood grain, tree bark, coins, manhole covers, lace, leaves, rough concrete. This is a direct transfer of real-world texture, creating unpredictable organic patterns.

 

SECTION 3: DIGITAL TEXTURE TECHNIQUES (Simulation and Efficiency)

Digital art must simulate texture through brushes, layers, and photographic textures.

3.1 The Custom Brush (The Digital Dry Brush)

Grain / Noise: Adding noise filter to brush tip makes strokes look like paper tooth.
Dual Brush: Combining two brush tips creates complex organic strokes.
Scattering: Randomizing brush tip position along stroke – use for foliage, stars, dust, freckles, gravel.
Pro Tip: Search for specialized brushes, but don't just stamp them. Vary size, opacity, angle. Hand-paint over stamped texture to break up obvious repetition.

3.2 Layer Modes and Texture Overlays (The Photobash Approach)

  1. Find a high-resolution photo texture (rusty metal, cracked mud, old leather).
  2. Place it over your painting layer.
  3. Set Layer Mode to Overlay, Soft Light, or Multiply.
  4. Adjust Opacity (usually 20-50%).
  5. Mask out areas where you don't want the texture.
  6. Critical Step: Hand-paint shadows and highlights over the texture to make it wrap around the 3D form.

3.3 Edge Wear and Grunge Maps (The "Used" Look)

  1. Create a Layer Mask on your object.
  2. Alt-Click the mask to enter it.
  3. Apply Clouds Filter (Render > Clouds).
  4. Apply Threshold Adjustment (Image > Adjustments > Threshold). This creates random jagged black/white pattern.
  5. Exit the mask – edges are now "eaten away".
  6. Paint manually into the mask to add specific scratches and dents.
  7. Result: Chipped paint, worn leather, battle-damaged armor.

SECTION 5: TEXTURE IN CONTEXT – The Principle of Material Differentiation

In a scene with multiple objects, varying the textures makes the image feel rich and believable. Mistake: everything looks like the same "stuff". Solution: The 3-Texture Rule. Every scene or character design should have at least three distinct material textures. Example: matte chainmail + glossy polished steel + worn leather glove. Squint test: can you see clear difference in value range and edge quality between materials?

 

SECTION 6: COMMON TEXTURE MISTAKES & HOW TO FIX THEM

MistakeWhy It HappensThe Fix
The "Wallpaper" EffectApplying texture brush evenly over flat shape without regard to lighting.Mask it. Erase texture where light hits directly and where shadow is deep. Texture lives in midtones.
The "Spaghetti" HairDrawing every single strand of hair individually.Draw clumps first, then add a few breakaway strands at silhouette edge.
The "Plastic" SkinBlending everything perfectly smooth with a soft airbrush.Add grain layer or noise filter at 5-10% opacity. Add specular hit on nose tip. Skin is slightly oily.
The "Mud" TextureUsing custom brush that is too soft and low-opacity, creating blurry mess.Use hard-edged brush with Opacity set to Pen Pressure. Build up texture in distinct passes.
The "Tile" RepetitionUsing photo texture that is too small, pattern visibly repeats.Clone Stamp to manually break up repeat. Use larger texture image. Rotate and scale multiple copies to hide seams.

 

SECTION 7: ASSIGNMENT / PRACTICE DRILL

Drill 1: The Texture Swatch Board (Traditional)

  • Divide a piece of paper into 12 squares.
  • In each square, draw a different texture using only pencil or pen (no words, no outlines of objects). Examples: Fur, Wood, Rock, Water Ripples, Woven Fabric, Fish Scales, Rusty Metal, Cracked Mud, Clouds, Grass, Leather, Skin Pores.
  • Goal: Build a personal visual library of mark-making.

Drill 2: The Frottage Hunt (Traditional)

  • Take thin paper and a graphite stick or crayon.
  • Find 10 different textures in your home or outside. Make a rubbing of each. Label them.
  • Goal: Realize texture is everywhere and can be directly captured.

Drill 3: The Sphere Material Study (Digital or Traditional)

  • Draw 5 identical spheres.
  • Render each sphere as a different material using only value and edge control: Shiny Metal, Rough Stone, Fuzzy Tennis Ball, Glass/Crystal, Wood.
  • Goal: Prove that material is defined by how it interacts with light, not by its colour.

Drill 4: The 50% Photo Overlay (Digital)

  • Paint a simple sword or rock shape using flat colours.
  • Find a high-resolution photo texture (rust, stone, leather). Overlay it (Overlay mode at 40%).
  • Paint over it to add shadows and highlights that match the 3D form.
  • Goal: Learn the hybrid workflow of photobashing and hand-painting.

Drill 5: The Edge Eater (Digital)

  • Draw a smooth, perfect circle.
  • Use the Clouds + Threshold masking technique (Section 3.3) to "destroy" the edge.
  • Paint additional scratches and gouges manually into the mask.
  • Goal: Understand procedural texture generation and edge wear.

Texture is the poetry of the surface – master it, and your worlds become tangible.
