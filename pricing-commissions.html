PROFESSIONAL PRACTICE: PRICING & COMMISSIONS GUIDE

How to Value Your Work, Run Professional Commissions, and Build a Sustainable Art Business

 

Introduction: The Art of Business

You have developed your skills. You have curated a strong portfolio. You are ready to be paid for your work. But the transition from "artist who sometimes sells a piece" to "professional freelance artist" requires a new set of skills: business skills.

This lesson will not give you a magic formula that tells you exactly what to charge. No such formula exists. Pricing is a dynamic interplay of skill level, market demand, project complexity, and client budget. What this lesson will give you is a framework for making informed, confident pricing decisions and a step-by-step system for running commissions that protects both you and your client.

The goal is not just to make money. The goal is to build a sustainable practice where you are fairly compensated for your time, skill, and creative energy.

 

SECTION 1: THE PRICING MINDSET – From Hobbyist to Professional

Before you can set a price, you must address the internal narratives that undermine your ability to charge what you are worth.

1.1 The Three Toxic Beliefs About Money and Art

Toxic BeliefWhat It Sounds LikeProfessional Reframe
"I'm just lucky to get paid for doing what I love.""I should be grateful for any amount.""I provide a valuable, specialised service. My time and skill have market value."
"There's always someone cheaper.""If I quote my real rate, the client will go to Fiverr.""I compete on quality, reliability and professionalism, not price."
"I have no idea what to charge.""Um... $50? Is that too much?""I have a method for calculating my prices. I state my rates with confidence."

1.2 The "Value, Not Time" Principle

Clients do not buy hours. They buy solutions. They buy a compelling character design that makes their game stand out, a cherished family portrait that will hang for decades. Frame conversations around the value of the outcome, not the cost of labor.

1.3 The "This Is a Business" Boundary

Separate bank account, defined business hours (even if flexible), written agreements. Never start work without a written agreement.

 

SECTION 2: HOW TO CALCULATE YOUR PRICES – Three Methods

2.1 Method 1: The Hourly Rate (The Foundation)

Calculate minimum annual needs (monthly expenses × 12). Add business expenses and taxes (25-30%). Total Annual Target Income. Estimate billable hours (full-time creative: 20-25 billable hours/week × 48 weeks = 960-1200 hours). Hourly Rate = Annual Target / Billable Hours. Example: $40,000 / 1000 hours = $40/hour minimum. Professional rates typically $50-$150+/hour.

2.2 Method 2: Per-Project / Flat Fee

Project Fee = (Estimated Hours × Hourly Rate) × Complexity Buffer (1.2x to 1.5x). Example: 10 hours × $50 = $500 × 1.3 = $650 flat fee.

2.3 Method 3: Value-Based Pricing (Commercial/Licensing)

For clients who will use art to generate revenue. Consider usage rights, exclusivity, duration. Non-exclusive limited use: +20-50% of base fee. Exclusive full buyout: +300-500% of base fee. Example: $500 base + ($500 × 4) = $2,500 total.

 

SECTION 3: THE COMMISSION PROCESS – A Step-by-Step Workflow

3.1 Step 1: The Initial Inquiry (The Filter)

Use a professional response template asking for description, intended use, dimensions, timeline, and approximate budget. This filters serious clients and gives you the information you need.

3.2 Step 2: The Quote and Agreement

Formal quote document includes: project description, deliverables, timeline, number of revisions, total fee, payment schedule, usage rights summary, and link to full TOS.

3.3 Step 3: The Contract / Terms of Service (TOS)

Always have a written agreement. See Section 5 for a full TOS template.

3.4 Step 4: Deposit and Scheduling

Standard industry deposit: 50% non-refundable upfront. Use professional invoicing (PayPal Invoicing, Stripe, Square). Never start without deposit.

3.5 Step 5: Work-in-Progress (WIP) and Communication

Checkpoints: sketch approval (most important), value/color rough (optional), final render with watermark, then final payment and unwatermarked delivery.

3.6 Step 6: Final Delivery and Follow-Up

Deliver via Google Drive/Dropbox. Send thank-you note. Ask for testimonial and permission to share the work.

SECTION 5: TERMS OF SERVICE (TOS) TEMPLATE

1. General: By commissioning me, you agree to these terms.
2. Process and Timeline: Work begins upon 50% non-refundable deposit. Estimated timeline provided.
3. Revisions: Includes [e.g., two] sketch rounds and one minor final round. Additional revisions billed hourly.
4. Payment: 50% deposit, 50% upon completion before final file delivery.
5. Cancellation and Refunds: Deposit non-refundable. If I cannot complete, full refund.
6. Usage Rights and Copyright: Artist retains copyright. Client granted personal, non-commercial license. Commercial use requires separate license. No NFTs or AI training.
7. Client Responsibility: Provide clear references and timely feedback.
8. Limitation of Liability: Limited to total amount paid.

 

SECTION 6: INVOICING AND TAXES

Professional invoice must include: your name/contact, client name, invoice number, date, description, amount, due date, payment method. Use PayPal Invoicing, Stripe, Square, Wave. As a freelancer, set aside 25-30% of every payment for taxes. Keep receipts for business expenses (software, supplies, equipment, home office portion).

 

SECTION 7: HANDLING DIFFICULT SITUATIONS

Scope Creep: "That's outside our original agreement; it would be an additional $X."
Client won't pay final invoice: Never deliver unwatermarked file before final payment. Send polite reminders; if ghosted, move on.
Client "doesn't like it" at final stage: Refer to revision policy. Identify specific issues and address within agreed minor revisions.

SECTION 9: ASSIGNMENT / PRACTICE DRILL

Drill 1: Calculate Your Minimum Hourly Rate – Use the formula in Section 2.1. Write it down. This is your baseline.
Drill 2: Create Your TOS Document – Use the template in Section 5. Customise with your name, revision policy, usage rights. Publish on your portfolio site or save as PDF.
Drill 3: Write Your Inquiry Response Template – Save a professional email template for initial inquiries.
Drill 4: Price a Hypothetical Commission – Full-body character with detailed background for a book cover (commercial). Estimate hours, apply hourly rate, buffer, commercial licensing fee. Write down the total quote. Practice saying it with confidence.
Drill 5: Audit a Recent Sale – Calculate effective hourly rate (total fee / actual hours). Compare to minimum rate. What would you do differently next time?

Your art has value. Your time has value. Price with confidence and professionalism.
