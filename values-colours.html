LESSON 3: VALUES AND COLORS

Color Theory, Value Structure, and Light Sources

 

Introduction: Value Does the Work, Color Gets the Credit

There is a famous saying in painting: "Value does all the work, but color gets all the glory."

You can paint a face with completely unrealistic colors—green skin, purple shadows, orange highlights—and if the values (the lights and dark) are correct, the face will still look three-dimensional and recognizable. Conversely, if you paint a face with perfect "flesh tone" colors but the values are wrong, it will look flat, muddy, or like a cardboard cutout.

This lesson will teach you to see the world in black and white first, and then apply colors as an emotional and atmospheric enhancement.

 

SECTION 1: VALUE STRUCTURE – The Skeleton of Light

Value refers to how light or dark a color is, independent of its hue (red, blue, etc.). It is the gray scale version of an image.

 

1.1 The Value Scale

Imagine a ladder with 10 steps.

StepNameDescription
1WhiteThe brightest highlight (specular reflection).
2-3Light LightsAreas directly facing the light source.
4-5Mid tonesThe "local color" of the object, untouched by bright light or deep shadow.
6-7Dark HalftonesThe transition zone where form turns away from light.
8-9Core Shadow / Deep ShadowThe darkest part of the form shadow.
10Black / Ambient OcclusionThe point where two surfaces touch and no light can enter.

The 80/20 Rule for Beginners: Most beginners put 80% of their painting in the mid tones (4-6) and the result looks grey and washed out. Push your darks darker and your lights lighter. A successful image usually has a clear Light Family (1-4) and a clear Shadow Family (7-10).

 

1.2 The Five Elements of Light on Form

When light hits a sphere (or a cheek, or an apple), it creates five distinct zones. If you memorize these, you can render any object.

  1. Center Light / Highlight: The spot facing most directly toward the light source. On a shiny surface, this is a sharp specular reflection. On a matte surface (skin, cloth), it's a soft glow.

  2. Halftone: The area where the form is beginning to turn away from the light. Crucial Note: The Halftone belongs to the Light Family. Do not let it get as dark as the shadow.

  3. Core Shadow: The darkest part of the form shadow. This is the zone that receives neither direct light from the source nor reflected light from the environment. It usually runs like a band along the edge where the light stops hitting the object. This is the single most important edge for creating volume.

  4. Reflected Light: Light that has bounced off the floor, a wall, or a nearby object and is illuminating the shadow side of your object. This light is darker than the halftone. A common beginner mistake is to make the reflected light too bright, which "blows out" the shadow and destroys the form.

  5. Cast Shadow: The shadow the object throws onto the surface behind it. Hard edge near the object; soft edge farther away (penumbra).

 

1.3 Value Grouping: The Secret to Readability

Do not try to render every tiny bump with its own value scale. Group similar values together.

  • Example (Portrait): The entire shadow side of the face (Core Shadow + Reflected Light) should be one narrow value range (e.g., values 7-9).

  • Example (Landscape): The sky is almost always the lightest value in the scene (except at night). The ground is the middle value. Trees/objects are the darkest value.

The Squint Test: Squint your eyes until the image blurs. You should see 3 to 4 large, distinct shapes of value. If you see a chaotic mess of 20 different values, the piece will lack visual impact.

 

SECTION 2: LIGHT SOURCES – The Mood Director

The direction, size, and distance of the light source change the value structure entirely.

 

2.1 Hard Light vs. Soft Light

FeatureHard Light (Small Source)Soft Light (Large Source)
Source ExampleMidday Sun, Bare Bulb, SpotlightOvercast Sky, Window Light, Softbox
Shadow EdgeSharp, crisp edgeGradient, soft edge
Cast ShadowDark, distinct shapeLight, blurry, almost invisible
MoodDramatic, high contrast, graphicCalm, ethereal, gentle

Physics Rule: The larger the light source relative to the subject, the softer the light.

 

2.2 Common Lighting Directions (The Portrait Cheat Sheet)

  • Top Light (Midday Sun / Stage Light): Creates dark, dramatic eye socket shadows. The nose casts a shadow over the upper lip. Mood: Intense, mysterious, or oppressive.

  • Front Light (Camera Flash / Ring Light): Flattens the form. Shadows are hidden behind the object. Value range is narrow. Mood: Clinical, direct, or "deer in headlights."

  • Rembrandt Light (45° Angle from Above): Creates a triangle of light on the shadowed cheek. This is the most "classical" portrait lighting. It separates the face from the background with a crisp dark edge on one side.

  • Rim Light (Behind the Subject): The light source is behind the subject. The edges of the silhouette glow white/hot, but the front of the subject is in deep shadow. Mood: Heroic, epic, or moody silhouette.

  • Under-Lighting (Firelight / Flashlight under chin): Reverses the normal shadow patterns. The underside of the nose is bright. The brow ridge is in shadow. Mood: Spooky, unnatural, or campfire intimate.

 

2.3 Ambient Occlusion (The Darkest Dark)

Wherever two objects touch or come very close together, light cannot bounce in. This creates a very thin, very dark shadow line. This is called Ambient Occlusion (AO).

Where to put AO:

  • The crease where the nose meets the face.

  • The line where the lips meet.

  • The fold of the eyelid.

  • The armpit.

  • The point where the foot touches the floor.

Adding AO is the fastest way to "ground" a character in a scene. If a character looks like they are floating, check the contact shadow between the feet and the ground plane.

 

SECTION 3: COLOR THEORY – The Emotion of Light

Once you understand Value, you can layer Color on top.

 

3.1 The Three Dimensions of Color

Every color you pick on a digital wheel or mix on a palette has three properties. You must control all three to paint well.

  1. Hue: The name of the color family. (Red, Blue, Yellow-Green). "What color is it?"

  2. Saturation (Chroma): The intensity or purity of the color. High saturation = Neon bright. Low saturation = Grey/Muddy. "How colorful is it?"

  3. Value: How light or dark it is. "How bright is it in gray scale?"

The Killer Mistake: Painting shadows by just picking a darker hue (e.g., adding Black). Black kills saturation. If you add black to yellow, you get a sickly, dead olive green.

Better Approach: Shift the Hue and lower the Value. (e.g., Yellow light -> Purple/Blue shadow).

 

3.2 The Color Wheel and Color Schemes

Primary Colors (RYB Model for Artists): Red, Yellow, Blue.

Secondary Colors: Orange, Green, Purple.

Tertiary Colors: Red-Orange, Yellow-Green, etc.

The 4 Main Color Schemes for Composition:

  1. Monochromatic: One Hue, varying Saturation and Value. Use: Elegant, calming, or moody pieces. Forces the viewer to focus on value structure.

  2. Complementary: Colors opposite on the wheel (Red/Green, Blue/Orange, Yellow/Purple). Use: Maximum contrast and vibration. When placed side-by-side, they make each other look more intense. Pro Tip: Do not use 50/50 ratio. Use 80% of one color (the Dominant) and 20% of the complement (the Accent). Also, gray down one of the colors slightly to avoid eye strain.

  3. Analogous: Colors next to each other on the wheel (Blue, Blue-Green, Green). Use: Harmony, nature scenes, calm atmospheres. Very little contrast.

  4. Triadic: Three colors evenly spaced on the wheel (Red, Yellow, Blue). Use: Playful, cartoonish, or highly graphic work. Hard to balance; usually requires one color to be the "King" and the other two to be "Pawns."

 

3.3 Color Temperature: Warm vs. Cool

This is relative, not absolute. A "cool red" (crimson) is warm compared to a "warm blue."

  • Warm Colors (Red, Orange, Yellow): Advance toward the viewer. Associated with energy, sunlight, fire, danger.

  • Cool Colors (Blue, Green, Purple): Recede into the distance. Associated with calm, shadow, water, sky.

The Golden Rule of Lighting:

Warm Light = Cool Shadows.

Cool Light = Warm Shadows.

(This is true for naturalistic painting. If the sun is yellow/orange, the shadows will look blue/purple in comparison.)

 

3.4 Atmospheric Perspective (Aerial Perspective)

Color and Value change over distance due to particles in the air (moisture, dust).

Objects Far Away:

  • Value: Contrast decreases. Darks get lighter.

  • Saturation: Decreases. Colors become grayer/bluer.

  • Hue: Shifts toward the color of the sky (usually cool blue/violet).

Objects Close Up:

  • Value: High contrast (dark darks, bright lights).

  • Saturation: High (vibrant colors).

  • Hue: True local color.

Landscape Hack: If you want a mountain range to look massive, paint the front mountain dark and saturated, and the back mountains pale blue-gray.

 

SECTION 4: PRACTICAL APPLICATION – Palettes & Mixing

 

4.1 The "Zorn Palette" (Limited Palette Mastery)

Anders Zorn, a master Swedish painter, used essentially four colors to paint realistic skin and landscapes:

  • White (Titanium)

  • Yellow Ochre (Warm earth yellow)

  • Cadmium Red (Warm red)

  • Ivory Black (Cool blue-ish black)

Why this works: The limitation forces you to focus on Value and Temperature. You cannot rely on a crazy tube of purple to make a shadow. You have to mix the black with the red to make a cool, deep violet shadow. This palette teaches more about color than a 24-color set ever will.

 

4.2 Digital Color Picking (Avoiding Mud)

  • The Color Picker Trap: Sliding straight down the picker square (lowering brightness) to make a shadow.

  • The Solution: Hue Shift. When picking a Shadow, slide the wheel slightly toward Blue/Purple AND lower the brightness. When picking a Highlight, slide the wheel slightly toward Yellow/Orange AND raise the brightness.

 

4.3 Local Colors vs. Perceived Color

  • Local Color: The "true" color of an object in flat white light. (An apple is Red).

  • Perceived Color: The color your eye sees based on the light source and reflections.

Example: That red apple under a blue sky. The shadow side of the apple will look Purple (Red + Blue light). The highlight might be pure White (the sun).

Mantra: Paint the light, not the object.

 

SECTION 5: ASSIGNMENT / PRACTICE DRILL

Instructions for the student:

Drill 1: The 5-Value Sphere (Traditional or Digital)

  • Draw a circle.

  • Define a light source (Top Left).

  • Fill the circle using only 5 distinct values (White, Light Grey, Mid Grey, Dark Grey, Black).

  • No blending. Keep the shapes hard-edged planes.

  • Include: Highlight, Halftone, Core Shadow, Reflected Light, Cast Shadow.

  • Goal: Understand planar value structure before blending.

Drill 2: The Color Isolation Study

  • Find a photo of a face with interesting lighting (Rembrandt or Golden Hour).

  • Create two studies side-by-side.

    1. Left Study: Paint the face using only Grayscale (black and white). Focus on perfect value matching.
    2. Right Study: Paint the face in Color.

  • Compare them. Squint at the color version. Does the value structure match the grayscale version? If the color version looks "flat," the values are wrong.

Drill 3: The Complementary Speed Paint

  • Set a timer for 20 minutes.

  • Choose a Complementary Pair (e.g., Blue and Orange).

  • Paint a simple scene (a figure in a room) using ONLY those two colors + White.

  • Goal: To learn how to mix a full value range from two opposing hues.

Drill 4: The Atmospheric Perspective Gradient

  • Draw three identical tree silhouettes overlapping.

  • Front Tree: Dark Green/Black, High Saturation.

  • Middle Tree: Mid Green, Medium Saturation.

  • Back Tree: Pale Blue-Grey, Low Saturation.

  • Goal: See the immediate depth created by color and value alone.

Master value first, then color becomes intuitive. Squint, simplify, and paint with confidence.
